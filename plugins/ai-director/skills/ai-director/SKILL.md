# AI Director: Cinematography, Image Generation & Storytelling

## 1. Hyper-Realistic AI Image Generation

### Master Prompt Formula
Structure every prompt as a production brief using this order:

**Subject + Action + Camera movement + Lighting + Environment + Mood + Material and physics cues**

### Multi-Layered Composition
Build scenes in layers to create depth:
1. **Background** — establish the world
2. **Middle ground** — supporting context
3. **Foreground** — place the subject here

### Dynamic Environmental Physics
Describe how the environment interacts with the scene. Examples:
- Wind blowing through loose hair
- Rain droplets bouncing off shoulders
- Ripples spreading across water

### Material Physics & Textures
Describe observable behavior, not just item names:
- Visible skin pores, natural skin texture
- Silk rippling with movement
- Glass refractions catching light
- Fabric pulling at specific stress points

### Lighting as an Anchor
Specify real-world light sources to create natural shadows:
- "Golden hour side lighting"
- "Warm tungsten table lamp casting long shadows"
- "Harsh fluorescent overhead"
- "Backlit window with rim lighting"
- Include secondary sources: sunlight bouncing off water, neon reflecting on wet pavement

### Micro-Expressions
Never use generic emotion words. Describe the face mechanically:
- "Eyes somewhat narrowed with real warmth"
- "Lips pressed together, slight tension at the corners"
- "Faint furrow between the eyebrows"
- Add micro-actions: natural breathing, subtle head tilt, fingers adjusting fabric

### Introduce Imperfections
AI defaults to too-perfect outputs. Add:
- Film grain
- Shallow depth of field (subject sharp, background soft)
- Light leaks
- Dust motes in light beams
- Lens artifacts (chromatic aberration, vignette)
- Slight motion blur on moving elements

## 2. Cinematic Video & Storyboard Direction

### Composition Rules

**Rule of Thirds**
Place subjects or focal points on the intersecting third-lines for emotional impact.
Use center framing only when emphasizing authority, teaching, or perfect symmetry.

**Depth and Focus**
- Open aperture = shallow depth of field = subject separated from background
- Pull subjects away from walls
- Add out-of-focus elements in the foreground to create depth layers

**Leading Lines & Frames**
- Use natural lines (roads, fences, corridors) to draw the eye toward the subject
- Use windows, doorways, or arches as a "frame within a frame" to emphasize the subject or evoke isolation

---

### Shot Type Reference

| Shot | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Establishing / Wide | Reveal location, set context, show subject's relationship to environment |
| Full Shot | Capture body language |
| Medium / Cowboy Shot | Show mood, emotion, confidence |
| Close-Up | Expose emotion, force viewer focus |
| Extreme Close-Up | Raw, intense emotional exposure |
| Over the Shoulder | Build tension or connection in dialogue |
| POV | Anchor viewer to character's direct perspective |

---

### Camera Angle Reference

| Angle | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Low Angle | Character looks powerful, dominant |
| High / Top Angle | Character looks vulnerable, powerless |
| Dutch Angle (off-kilter) | Psychological imbalance, unease, danger |

---

### Camera Movement Reference

**Straight Line Moves**
- **Dolly In** — audience moves closer, character reaches realization
- **Dolly Out** — creates isolation, pulls world away
- **Pan** (horizontal) — reveals the environment left-to-right
- **Tilt** (vertical) — reveals scale up or down

**Curved / Aerial Moves**
- **Orbital / 180 or 360 Spin** — epic scale, shows full context
- **Drone / Aerial** — establishes massive scale, godlike perspective

**Impact Moves**
- **Crash Zoom** — snaps viewer attention suddenly

---

### Shot Control for Video Prompts (Animation)
For smooth AI video generation:
1. Define a **Start Frame** (hero image — the exact first frame)
2. Define an **End Frame** (where the camera or scene lands)
3. Maintain **temporal consistency** by repeating the character's clothing, hair, and spatial direction in every text prompt across the sequence

## 3. Storytelling Framework

### The Story Skeleton
Every narrative must move through this backbone:

**Identity → Conflict → Choice → Change**

- **Identity**: Who is the character right now, before anything happens?
- **Conflict**: What challenge disrupts their world?
- **Choice**: What decision do they face?
- **Change**: How are they different by the end?

A story is not a summary of events. It is a mechanism to convey **meaning and transformation**.

---

### Trailer / Storyboard Structure
Structure any visual narrative into four parts:

1. **Hook** — powerful visual or audio opening that demands attention
2. **Setup** — fast-paced character introduction, world established
3. **Story Building** — the most emotional and visually powerful moments
4. **Climax** — suspense builds, then explosive or resonant reveal

---

### The 5 Storytelling Elements
When writing a script or scene, zoom into the specific moment using all five:

1. **Location** — state exactly where the scene happens so the audience can visualize it ("sitting on my couch at 2am")
2. **Actions** — use physical verbs for forward momentum ("I open my laptop", "she slams the door")
3. **Thoughts** — share raw, unfiltered, slightly neurotic inner voice — not polished summaries
4. **Emotions** — don't name the emotion, show it on the body (pen tapping, a long exhale, jaw tightening)
5. **Dialogue** — include the exact, specific words spoken at the crucial moment

---

### Reliving vs. Reporting
Stop reporting: "I was nervous."
Start reliving: use present tense and the five senses.

- What did you **see**?
- What did you **hear**?
- What did you **smell** or **taste**?
- What did you **feel** physically?

This is what makes a story addictive instead of forgettable.

---

### Holding Attention
Inject these three mechanisms throughout any story or video:

- **Open Loops** — withhold information to create an information gap the audience needs to close
- **Pattern Breaking** — visual cuts, perspective shifts, unexpected tonal changes
- **Escalation** — raise the stakes continuously; each beat should cost more than the last

The story is the doorway. Always deliver the **meaning** — the perspective shift that makes the narrative relatable and memorable.
